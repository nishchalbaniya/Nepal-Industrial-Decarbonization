## Post 1 — "What EU CBAM means for your cement plant (and what to do about it)"

> **SEO target:** "EU CBAM cement Nepal", "CBAM impact Nepali cement
> exporters", "carbon border adjustment Nepal 2026".
> **Persona:** Plant Manager Ramesh.
> **Format:** 500 words. Includes a 1-table "CBAM cost calculator"
> for the 6 Nepali cement plants (using their published capacities
> and the 783 kg/t intensity from our PlantA pilot as a
> placeholder) — readers can copy the table into Excel and plug in
> their own numbers.

**Summary.** The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism is no longer
a "coming" thing — it is in its definitive phase, and any bag of
Nepali cement (or clinker) that lands at Rotterdam or Hamburg
after 2026 is being assessed a carbon tariff equal to the
difference between the EU ETS price and the verified embedded
emissions of the product. For a PlantA-class plant with an
intensity of 783 kg CO₂/t cement, the math at €80/tCO₂ is roughly
**€32 per tonne of exported cement** — a cost that comes straight
off the price your Indian and Bangladeshi competitors (who already
report) do not pay. This post walks through (1) what CBAM actually
requires you to disclose (the embedded emissions, the methodology,
the verification body), (2) what "no measurement" costs you in
practice when the EU asks for numbers you cannot produce, and (3)
the three things you can do in the next 90 days to be ready:
compute a Tier 2 baseline, get a PlantA-style ISO 14064-1
100/100 report, and have a VVB lined up for the first
verification. We use the PlantA pilot numbers as the worked
example, but the post is explicitly a guide any plant manager can
follow. End with: "If you want the same template we used for
PlantA, it's free at nepalcarbon.org.np/signup — you'll have
your first baseline by the end of the week." CTA: signup.

## Post 2 — "How PlantA can make $22.5M from carbon credits"

> **SEO target:** "carbon credits cement Nepal", "Verra credits
> cement", "PlantA emissions", "VM0009 cement".
> **Persona:** Owner-Operator Sita (and any Nepali plant owner who
> sees the headline and wants to know if it applies to them).
> **Format:** 500 words + 1 chart (the emissions waterfall) + 1
> embed of the live PlantA dashboard screenshot. Tag: case study.

**Summary.** A $22.5M number sounds like a typo until you walk
through the math, so this post does the walk-through in plain
language. PlantA Industries Ltd (Makwanpur, 1.1 Mt/yr
capacity, dry process) has a measured 2024 baseline of **861,025
tCO₂/yr** and an intensity of **783 kg CO₂/t cement**. Apply a
realistic project scenario — biomass co-firing replacing 15% of
coal, plus a 5 MW waste-heat-recovery unit — and the gross annual
reduction is 69,854 tCO₂. After the Verra buffer (15%) and
leakage (5%), the **net annual credits are 56,407 tCO₂/yr**. At
the EU ETS secondary-market price of $65/t, that is **$3.67M/year
in revenue**, and over the standard 10-year Verra crediting
period, the NPV is **$22.5M**. The post is honest about the
assumptions (the project scenario costs ~$8M capex, the VVB
verification is ~$25k/year, the additionality test is real), and
it is clear that the first mover gets the easiest credit
issuance. CTA: download the free PlantA baseline report (PDF,
1-page executive summary, no email gate).

**Summary.** ISO 14064-1 is the standard every bank, every buyer,
and every carbon market asks for, and most Nepali plant managers
think it requires a foreign consultant and a 6-month project. It
doesn't. The standard has 20 criteria, they are the same 20
criteria every time, and if you have one year of monthly
production and fuel data, you can answer all 20 in a single
afternoon. The post gives the **3-step plan**: **(1) collect**
the 12-month data in our free CSV template (clinker, cement, coal,
petcoke, electricity, limestone, clay, iron ore), **(2) compute**
the baseline using IPCC Tier 2 mass-balance (one click in our
free tool, or in any spreadsheet if you want to do it by hand),
**(3) document** the 20 criteria using the checklist (boundary,
methodology, base year, uncertainty, audit trail). The PlantA
pilot scored **100/100 on all 20 criteria** in the live
platform, and the same platform is free to use for any Nepali
plant. CTA: download the free template + checklist (no email

## Post 4 — "Why your Clamp kiln is losing you money (and the alternative)"

> **SEO target:** "clamp kiln Nepal", "brick kiln upgrade Nepal",
> "zigzag kiln Nepal", "tunnel kiln Nepal", "UNEP brick kiln
> Nepal".
> **Persona:** Owner-Operator Sita (primary), Department of
> Environment (secondary).
> **Format:** 500 words + 1 photo of a Nepali clamp kiln (with
> permission) + 1 table comparing clamp / zigzag / tunnel /
> Hoffman on emissions, fuel use, capex, and NPV.

**Summary.** About 70% of Nepal's 1,200 active brick kilns are
still traditional **clamp kilns** — the open-pile, fixed-chimney
design that UNESCO lists as one of the world's most polluting
industrial technologies. A clamp kiln fires 1 tonne of
clay into 1 tonne of bricks using roughly 25–30 MJ of energy and
emits 200–250 kg CO₂ per 1,000 bricks, with a fuel-to-product
efficiency of 25–35%. A **zigzag kiln** cuts fuel by 30–40%
and emissions by 40%, with a capex of NPR 8–15 lakh and a
payback of 14–22 months. A **tunnel kiln** cuts fuel by 50%+ and
emissions by 60%+, but the capex is NPR 1.5–3 crore and only
makes sense at 30,000+ bricks/day scale. The post is written for
Sita, who runs a family clamp kiln in Bhaktapur, and it walks
through her three realistic next steps: (1) join the next NCMA /
UNEP / GEF cohort that subsidises zigzag conversion, (2) get her
current kiln onto a free MRV platform so she can quantify
emissions and access results-based climate finance, (3) join a
producer cooperative to amortise a tunnel kiln across 4–5 owners.
CTA: signup for the free brick-kiln baseline + an invite to our
monthly brick-kiln owner webinar (in नेपाली).
